Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji

Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji, the ninth Sikh Guru, served from 1621 to 1675 and was renowned for his profound spiritual understanding and exceptional courage. His life and impact are significant in Sikh history for a variety of reasons. His term as Guru began amid exceptional circumstances. Following the death of Guru Har Krishan, the former Guru, there was some ambiguity concerning his replacement. Many imposters went to Bakala, claiming the title of Guru. During this period, Makhan Shah, a merchant who had survived a violent storm at sea, agreed to give the Guru 501 gold pieces if his life was spared. When Makhan Shah arrived in Bakala, he tested each claimant by providing merely two coins, knowing that the actual Guru would honour his word. When he reached Guru Tegh Bahadur, the Guru instantly sought the remaining money to authenticate his identity. Overjoyed, Makhan Shah proudly announced the finding of the genuine Guru from the rooftops.

Guru Tegh Bahadur made great contributions to Sikhism. He included various songs in the Sikh scriptures, which were ultimately included into the Guru Granth Sahib by his son, Guru Gobind Singh. This amendment was incorporated into the final compilation of the Sikh texts, which has remained unmodified since. The most moving part of Guru Tegh Bahadur's life was his sacrifice, which demonstrated his unwavering dedication to the ideas of religious freedom and justice. He was contacted by Kashmiri Brahmins who were being compelled to convert to Islam under the reign of Mughal king Aurangzeb. Guru Tegh Bahadur chose to advocate for their right to religious freedom, which resulted in his detention. When given the option of converting to Islam or facing execution, he chose the latter and was killed in Delhi in 1675. His sacrifice was intended to safeguard religious freedom and liberty, not only for his followers but for all religions.

Following Guru Tegh Bahadur execution, Guru Tegh Bahadur body and head were taken away by dedicated Sikhs under perilous conditions. Lakhi Shah Vanjara, a Sikh, recovered the Guru's body and set fire to his whole house in order to execute the funeral rituals without raising suspicion. Meanwhile, three lower-caste Sikhs known as Ranghreta Sikhs recovered the Guru's head. They returned it to Anandpur Sahib, where Guru Gobind Singh and the Sikh community lamented its loss. This gesture gained the Ranghreta community a high standing in Sikh culture. Guru Tegh Bahadur's martyrdom has long been a symbol of ultimate sacrifice, reinforcing the Sikh community's dedication to preserving their faith and the rights of all individuals to practise their religion freely. His life and teachings continue to inspire millions throughout the world, exhibiting the real spirit of resisting injustice and advocating for justice and fairness.
