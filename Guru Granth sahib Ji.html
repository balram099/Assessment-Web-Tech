Guru Granth Sahib Ji

Guru Gobind Singh Ji was a pivotal figure in Sikh history, taking on the mantle of Guru at a young age in 1675. He's known for his bravery, spiritual depth, and visionary leadership. His contributions to Sikhism are profound, including the founding of the Khalsa, a community of committed Sikhs, in 1699. This was a transformative moment, marking Sikhs not just by their faith but also by a distinct identity and way of life. Guru Gobind Singh Ji was also a poet and a warrior, embodying the dual path of spirituality and temporal duty. The Siri Guru Granth Sahib, the holy book of Sikhism, is a vast collection of 1,430 pages that includes teachings and songs. Guru Gobind Singh Ji played a crucial role in its history, declaring it as the eternal Guru of the Sikhs in 1708. This sacred text contains contributions from six Sikh Gurus and 30 saints from diverse backgrounds, making it a rich tapestry of spiritual wisdom. Its verses are set to 60 different Raags, or musical scales, designed to be sung at specific times of day or seasons, enriching the Sikh worship experience.

This holy book begins with the Japji Sahib by Guru Nanak and features writings from the first five Sikh Gurus and the ninth, Guru Teg Bahadur. Guru Arjan, who compiled it initially, also included verses from Muslim and Hindu saints, showcasing the inclusive spirit of Sikhism. On October 20, 1706, Guru Gobind Singh Ji formally installed the Siri Guru Granth Sahib as the Guru, a tradition celebrated annually as "Guru Gaddi Day." Upon his death in 1708, Guru Gobind Singh Ji passed the spiritual leadership of the Sikhs to the Siri Guru Granth Sahib, ending the era of physical Gurus. He proclaimed that henceforth, the Sikh community should seek guidance from the Guru Granth Sahib, the embodiment of the Word of God. This marked the beginning of the Shabad Guru's reign, guiding Sikhs as a living spirit of enlightenment.

The Guru Granth Sahib, also known as the Adi Granth, is more than simply a scripture; it is the Sikhs' living Guru. It is a unique religious scripture that incorporates the teachings of Sikh Gurus and saints from many backgrounds, emphasising God's oneness while rejecting caste and superstition. Its legitimacy and comprehensiveness are unrivalled, having been compiled by Sikh Gurus themselves. It is honoured in Sikh temples, or Gurdwaras, as a sign of its importance in Sikh devotion and communal life. The Guru Granth Sahib is written in Gurmukhi, a script derived from Sanskrit, which helps to connect the Sikh faith to its spiritual and cultural roots.
