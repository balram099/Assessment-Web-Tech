Guru Granth Sahib

The Guru Granth Sahib, also known as the Adi Granth or Adi Sri Granth Sahib Ji, holds a revered status in Sikhism beyond that of a mere scripture. It is considered the eternal living Guru, following the ten human Gurus of the Sikh faith. Compiled first by the fifth Sikh Guru, Arjan Dev, in 1604 and later finalized by Guru Gobind Singh in 1705, this sacred text encompasses 1430 pages of hymns and teachings from Sikhismâ€™s founders.

In 1708, Guru Gobind Singh, the last of the human Gurus, formally bestowed Guruship on the Guru Granth Sahib, commanding all Sikhs to accept the Granth as their everlasting Guru. This directive led Sikhs to recognise an eleventh Guru â€” the Guru Granth Sahib itself. Unique among major world religious scriptures, the Guru Granth Sahib includes writings from diverse saints across Hinduism and Islam, particularly those who challenged societal norms like caste divisions and championed the unity of God. This compilation was carried out by the Gurus themselves, ensuring the authenticity and integrity of its teachings.

Visitors to a Gurdwara, the Sikh place of worship, will find the Guru Granth Sahib prominently displayed in the Darbar Sahib or main hall. It rests on a raised platform, draped with beautifully colored cloths, and sheltered by a decorative canopy, underscoring its sacred status. The scripture is written in Gurmukhi script, a derivation of Sanskrit meaning "from the Guruâ€™s mouth." The Guru Granth Sahib stands out for its inclusive and ecumenical nature, incorporating the voices of Hindu bhaktas, Muslim divines, and Sufi poets alongside Sikh Gurus. This diversity reflects Guru Arjan Devâ€™s vision of a spiritual unity across different faiths. The text not only offers spiritual guidance but also reflects the social, economic, and political landscapes of 12th to 17th century India, critiquing oppressive rulers and corrupt religious leaders while promoting a message of equality and spiritual integrity.

Dasam Granth

The Dasam Granth, also held in high regard within Sikhism, is an extensive text attributed to Guru Gobind Singh, the tenth Sikh Guru. Unlike the Guru Granth Sahib, the primary religious scripture of Sikhism, the Dasam Granth is a compilation encompassing a diverse array of writings that explore various facets of spirituality and morality. This scripture comprises a wide variety of literary works, including religious hymns, mythological narratives, and philosophical treatises. Noteworthy sections within its pages are the Jaap Sahib, Akal Ustat, and Bachittar Natak. The Jaap Sahib is an emphatic hymn extolling the omnipotence and attributes of God, underscoring the divine as beyond human understanding. Akal Ustat celebrates the timeless and formless one, reflecting on the eternal nature of God. The Bachittar Natak, meaning "Wonderful Drama," is an autobiographical piece that recounts the lineage of Guru Gobind Singh, highlighting the spiritual conflicts faced by his ancestors and himself.

The Dasam Granth includes the Chandi di Var, a ballad depicting the battles of the goddess Durga against demons, symbolising the struggle against evil and injustice. This text is frequently recited in Sikh martial arts (Gatka) settings to inspire moral and physical fortitude. The compositions within the Dasam Granth are integral to Sikh liturgy. For instance, various hymns from this Granth are recited daily by devout Sikhs as part of their morning prayers. Moreover, its themes of divine sovereignty and heroism resonate profoundly with the Khalsa, the community of initiated Sikhs founded by Guru Gobind Singh. The Dasam Granth is not merely a religious text but also a treasure trove of Punjabi literature, showcasing the linguistic and poetic prowess of Guru Gobind Singh. Its contents reflect a fusion of devotion, bravery, and philosophical insight, continuing to inspire Sikhs and scholars across the globe.

Sikh Poetry

Sikh poetry is a vital and vibrant part of Sikh literature, offering a broad spectrum of styles ranging from devotional verses and heroic ballads to deep philosophical musings. This rich literary form is integral to understanding the Sikh community's core values and spiritual ethos. Devotional verses in Sikh poetry often focus on the love for and devotion to a singular, omnipresent God. These verses aim to evoke a sense of deep spiritual connection and personal surrender to the divine will, encouraging a life led by faith and total trust in the divine plan.

Heroic ballads, another prominent feature of Sikh poetry, recount the valorous deeds of Sikh Gurus and martyrs. These ballads serve to inspire courage and righteousness, celebrating the bravery and sacrifices made by these figures in the face of oppression. They also reinforce the warrior aspect of Sikh identity, emphasizing the principle of 'Saint Soldier' or 'Sant Sipahi', which advocates for righteousness armed with both spiritual and worldly wisdom. Philosophical musings in Sikh poetry delve into questions of existence, the nature of God, and the path to liberation. These poems often reflect on the human condition, the cycle of life and death, and the pursuit of truth. By engaging with these themes, Sikh poetry promotes a reflective and contemplative approach to life, urging individuals to seek a higher consciousness and to recognise the unity of all creation. Together, these various forms of poetry not only enrich the cultural tapestry of Sikhism but also serve as an educational tool, helping to impart the teachings of the Sikh Gurus. They foster a sense of community and continuity within the faith, linking the present with the past through shared stories and common aspirations. By celebrating the universal and inclusive message of Sikhism, this poetry continues to resonate with followers, encouraging them to live lives of courage, contemplation, and devotion.
