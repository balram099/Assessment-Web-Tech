Guru Arjan Dev Ji

The fifth Sikh Guru, Guru Arjan (1563–1606), was crucial to the political and spiritual growth of Sikhism. Significant obstacles faced him during his term, particularly from within his own family, as his older brother Prithi Chand angrily disagreed with his leadership. The strife among the family took a perilous turn when Prithi Chand tried to poison Hargobind, the infant son of Guru Arjan. Furthermore, Prithi Chand and his adherents disseminated phoney hymns under the false pretence that they were the compositions of previous Gurus in an effort to further undermine Arjan's authority. Guru Arjan took up the enormous effort of assembling the Sikh texts in response to these challenges and to protect the authenticity of Sikh teachings. He used Bhai Gurdas as his scribe and used the manuscripts known as the Goindval Pothis, which were composed under the guidance of his forebears, as his main source. The Adi Granth, which underwent more modifications, became the Guru Granth Sahib. This compilation was the outcome of the endeavour. Sikhs look to this scripture, considered as the everlasting Guru, for direction, consolation, and spiritual comfort.

Under his guidance, the Sikh community was greatly extended, particularly among the Jat agricultural caste, which resulted in a notable rise in the number of converts. However, the Mughal authorities were disturbed by his successful outreach attempts. Rumours that Guru Arjan was backing his rebellious son Khusro influenced Emperor Jahangir, and the Mughal ruler of Punjab became more and more alarmed about the spread of Sikhism. The result of these political tensions was Guru Arjan's arrest and severe torture at the hands of Mughal authorities. Guru Arjan had a significant influence on Sikhism's spiritual and architectural environment prior to his demise. He oversaw the building of Amritsar's Harmandir Sahib (Golden Temple), which would eventually grow to be considered the most sacred place in Sikhism. Beyond religious literature and temples, he also founded mass langars (community kitchens) that welcomed individuals from all castes and backgrounds, fostering equality and a sense of community.

Not only was Guru Arjan's passing heartbreaking, but it also marked a turning point in Sikhism. His dying directive to his son Hargobind was to always carry a gun, opening the door for the Sikh community to take a more militant stand for justice and their faith. With this guidance, Sikh history entered a new era in which spiritual devotion and military readiness converged to shape the path of the Sikh faith under succeeding Gurus.
