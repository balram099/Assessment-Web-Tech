Guru Gobind Singh Ji

Guru Gobind Singh ji, born in 1666 in Patna, Bihar, was the tenth and last personal Guru of the Sikhs, serving from 1675 till Guru Gobind Singh ji death in 1708 in Nanded, Maharashtra. He was a multidimensional leader known for founding the Khalsa, a military fraternity among Sikhs, as well as being a superb linguist and military strategist. Guru Gobind Singh was fluent in Persian, Arabic, Sanskrit, and Punjabi and wrote the Dasam Granth, an important work in Sikh literature. Guru Gobind Singh, the son of Guru Tegh Bahadur, the ninth Guru who was killed by the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb, was formed by Guru Gobind Singh ji father's sacrifice. He was raised in Anandpur and had significant training in both military disciplines and classical languages. Guru Gobind Singh ji early years were defined by involvement in regional wars and a love of hunting, which demonstrated Guru Gobind Singh ji abilities as both a scholar and a fighter.

During the Baisakhi festival in Anandpur in 1699, Guru Gobind Singh oversaw one of the most crucial occurrences in Sikh history. In a spectacular ceremony, he summoned volunteers from among Guru Gobind Singh ji followers to walk up and give their lives for their beliefs. This test culminated in the foundation of the Panj Piare, or "Cherished Five," who exhibited unwavering commitment and became the Khalsa's founding members. This new order represented a dramatic shift in the Sikh community, emphasising purity, loyalty, and martial spirit. The Khalsa was meant to eradicate corruption and middlemen in Sikh spiritual leadership, so forging a direct relationship between Sikhs and their Guru. In addition to founding the Khalsa, Guru Gobind Singh had a tremendous impact on Sikhism's military and spiritual traditions. He ordered Khalsa Sikhs to wear guns and keep their hair uncut, symbolising their willingness to defend their faith and distinct identity. Although the wearing of the "Five Ks" (kes, kangha, kachha, kara, and kirpan) was subsequently formalised, Guru Gobind Singh established these rituals to strengthen Guru Gobind Singh ji disciples' dedication and discipline.

Guru Gobind Singh ji literary works include the 'Dasam Granth,' a collection of manuscripts attributed to him, including hymns and philosophical essays. These writings are renowned for their rich language and lyrical structure, which offer profound insights into Guru Gobind Singh ji spiritual philosophy and the values of a virtuous life. Guru Gobind Singh's personal life was marred by tragedy, with the deaths of all four of Guru Gobind Singh ji sons two in combat and two killed by Mughal authorities. These events constitute a sombre chapter in Sikh history, demonstrating the ultimate sacrifices committed for their religion.

Guru Gobind Singh ji last years were spent in the Deccan, where he completed Guru Gobind Singh ji father's work on the modification of the Adi Granth. After Aurangzeb's death, he briefly collaborated with Guru Gobind Singh ji successor, Bahadur Shah, but was eventually slain in 1708 by those opposed to Guru Gobind Singh ji reforms and military operations.
