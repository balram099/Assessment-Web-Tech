Guru Amardas Ji

Through his reforms and leadership, the third Guru of Sikhism, Guru Amar Das, had a profound impact on the Sikh community. Born in 1479, he converted to the Sikh faith after a life-changing spiritual encounter and assumed the role of Guru at the age of 73 upon the passing of Guru Angad. Spiritual and institutional developments during his term as Guru bolstered and cemented the Sikh community. A dedicated lifestyle free from the ceremonial procedures common in other religious traditions of the period was stressed by Guru Amar Das. He was a strong supporter of equality and fiercely opposed to gender discrimination and caste divisions. Promoting the Langar, a communal meal that is available to everyone regardless of caste, race, or gender, was one of his most significant accomplishments. This practice emphasised the Sikh ideas of equality and community.

Significant literary contributions to Sikhism were also made by Guru Amar Das. Nine hundred and seven of his hymns were eventually included in the Guru Granth Sahib. The themes of devotion to God, the pointlessness of rituals, and the significance of the Guru in a devotee's spiritual life persisted throughout his songs. Guru Amar Das created new, Sikh-focused pilgrimage sites as a calculated attempt to strengthen the Sikh identity and offer an alternative to the established Hindu pilgrimage sites. The most important of them was the baoli (stepwell) at Goindval, which developed into a social hub for the Sikh community in addition to being a place of worship.

The introduction and formalisation of many Sikh festivals by Guru Amar Das contributed to the development of stronger ties within the community. These included the spring harvest celebration of Baisakhi and the festive celebration of Diwali, which unites Sikh celebrations with the wider cultural customs of the area while maintaining their unique Sikh importance. In the Sikh community, he was a fervent supporter of women's equality and rights. He forbade customs like Sati, which is the burning of a widow on her husband's funeral pyre, and Purdah, which is the veiling of women, and he promoted women's involvement in all religious and social Sikhism-related activities.
