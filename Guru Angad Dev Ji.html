Guru Angad Dev Ji

After Guru Nanak Dev Ji, the second Sikh Guru was Guru Angad Dev Ji, who was born in Sarai Matta, India, as Lahina. Daya Kaur Ji and Pheru Mall Ji were his parents when he was born. After he married Mata Khivi, his life changed dramatically. Together, they produced two boys, Dassu Ji and Dattu Ji, and two daughters, Bibi Amro Ji and Bibi Anokhi Ji. Guru Nanak's teachings and songs had a profound impact on Guru Angad. He was a devoted Hindu before converting to Sikhism, and he worshipped the goddess Durga. While organising a trip to the Javalamukhi Temple in Himachal Pradesh, his spiritual journey really got underway. He met Guru Nanak on this journey in the vicinity of Kartarpur, and the holy hymns and Nanak's spiritual presence left a deep impression on him. His life's course was altered by this encounter, which made him a committed follower of Guru Nanak.

Angad means "part of a body," signifying Lahina's essential position in Sikhism. Guru Nanak named Lahina as his successor after recognising his profound devotion to and comprehension of the teachings. The leadership of Guru Angad was characterised by his humility and commitment to carrying out Guru Nanak's vision. Among the many important things Guru Angad did for Sikhism was to design the Gurmukhi script. This script was essential for recording the Sikh Gurus' hymns and making sure the general public could understand their teachings. Gurmukhi, meaning "from the mouth of the Guru," contributed to the language's standardisation and preservation, which made studying Sikh scriptures simpler for next generations. This script was made to phonetically transcribe a variety of regional dialects rather than being restricted to any one language.

The langar, a communal kitchen where food is offered to everyone regardless of caste, creed or economic background, is a practice that Guru Angad encouraged. With the active assistance of his wife, Mata Khivi, Guru Angad extended what Guru Nanak had originally initiated. Her contribution to the planning of these dinners was significant, as they developed into a focal point for fostering equality and a sense of community among Sikhs. During his time as a guru, Guru Angad worked to uplift society, instil the principles of equality, humility, and service, and encourage his followers to engage in physical and spiritual activities like wrestling (Mal Akhara). His leadership was a living example of the core Sikh values of commitment, service, and continuity.
