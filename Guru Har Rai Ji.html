Guru Har Rai Ji

Guru Har Rai was the seventh Sikh Guru from 1630 to 1661, a very peaceful time. He chose a more private existence, relocating deeper into the Shiwalik Hills to a town named Sirmur. Despite his isolated existence, he maintained contact with his followers by leaving his hideaway on occasion to visit and preach to Sikhs on the Punjab plains. Masands, trustworthy emissaries who transmitted news and collected donations to support the Sikh faith's operations, helped him communicate with the larger Sikh community. This calm time, however, was not without obstacles. Guru Har Rai's term coincided with substantial political instability in the Mughal Empire. After conquering his brother Dara Shikoh and establishing the Mughal throne, Aurangzeb attempted to preserve allegiance. Aurangzeb, who saw Guru Har Rai as a supporter of his opponent, requested that he transfer his son, Ram Rai, to the Mughal court. The underlying purpose was probably twofold: to utilise Ram Rai as a political captive and to shape him into a Mughal supporter.

During Ram Rai's stay in the Mughal court, he faced a critical episode that questioned his honesty. Aurangzeb questioned a passage in Sikh texts that he construed as insulting to Muslims. Under pressure, Ram Rai changed the phrasing to avoid insulting the Emperor. Guru Har Rai saw the act of changing the scripture as a serious mistake. According to Sikh tradition, the words of the Guru Granth Sahib are sacred, and changing them is considered sacrilege. Disturbed by his son's activities, Guru Har Rai promised never to meet Ram Rai again, severing relations with him for jeopardising the purity of Sikh teachings. As a result, Ram Rai did not take up the job of Guru. Instead, the Sikh community's leadership was passed down to Har Rai's younger son, Hari Krishen, who was just five years old. This shift highlighted the primary ideals of Sikhism: fidelity to the Gurus' unmodified teachings and the moral rectitude demanded of its leaders. Guru Har Rai's leadership therefore emphasised both a period of relative tranquilly and a watershed moment of principled resistance to foreign influences and internal problems, guaranteeing the integrity and continuation of the Sikh faith.
